# 理性与修辞能力

如果我们无法找到一套在所有传播情境中都能反映人类选择的、恒定不变的价值等级体系，是否就意味着每一次传播互动都必然会、也必须会由一组独特且个性化的价值所支配？我认为并非如此。至少在西方社会，**理性**在任何自称或被视为修辞性的传播中都是一项超越性价值。据此，我认为理性是修辞能力的本质属性。修辞能力至少意味着：一个人能够构思传播内容、创作恰当的话语、根据传播媒介、受众与情境以合适的方式呈现信息，并能分析和评估自己与他人的信息。要在理解实质、方法与互动关系的基础上完成这些事，就必须掌握我所提出的**好理由的逻辑**。一个人若无法辨识特定修辞情境中所表达或拟表达的价值的存在性、相关性、后果与一致性，就不可能具备修辞能力。

**通情达理**更多体现为一种态度，而非有效参与传播互动的能力。我并非认为通情达理不是修辞互动的重要组成部分。我赞同佩雷尔曼的立场：通情达理是多元世界观的前提，是将理性转化为人类心智实践的力量。民主人格的观念——这是非一元化社会的基本预设——正是由通情达理与理性这两个概念共同构成的。正如佩雷尔曼所指出的，如果理性被理解为对形式逻辑的遵从，其大前提由固定的治理理念所规定，结果便会形成一元论体系，即封闭社会。理性会沦为保守主义的附庸。而我所提出的理性概念，则使其成为推动理性变革的工具。²⁴

通情达理是理性的一个方面，而非其核心内涵。说一个人在民主体制中通情达理，是指他会运用理由，并尊重、包容其他以理由证成自身观点的人。而**具备理性**则不仅意味着尊重推理，还表明一个人了解论辩议题的性质、论证形式及其检验标准，并且知晓在特定论辩互动中所遵循的规则——无论这一场域是科学、政治、法律、伦理还是其他领域。一个人可以通情达理却能力不足，但不可能具备理性却能力匮乏。

为进一步厘清这一区别，我想考察几组论辩互动。第一种情境：甲方断言，“死刑就是谋杀，一清二楚。”乙方回应，“才不是！死刑是正当报应。讲点道理。”甲方反驳道，“你说讲点道理是什么意思？你才不讲道理。”从中我们可以看到大众观念中常见的争论形式：言语与情绪上的对立。没有任何陈述用来证成主张，争论双方的矛盾转向人身攻击，而非实质议题。事实上，要求对方“讲点道理”所隐含的信息无非是：提出观点的人要么（1）无知，不了解事件事实；要么（2）愚钝，掌握事实却不知如何从中得出恰当推论；要么（3）掌握事实、推理无误，但出发点或前提本身有误。我所描述的这种情境显然是**不讲道理**的。它不具备可持续性，带有操控性，无任何实质意义上的双向性，也不具备商议性、反思性，更不关注事实依据。

第二种互动情境：甲方说，“死刑是不道德的，它违背了‘不可杀人’的戒律。”乙方回答，“你的立场很合理，但我不认同。我认为死刑是一种公共行为，而非个人违背国家法律的行为。从‘以眼还眼’的准则来看，它在道德上是正当的。”甲方回应，“我明白你的意思，但我不认为在剥夺他人生命这件事上，国家行为与个人行为存在区别。”乙方反驳，“好吧，我想我们只能求同存异了。我看不出有什么办法能解决我们的分歧。”

这场对话体现了**通情达理**：双方都为自己的主张提供了依据，尊重彼此的论证，没有将冲突个人化。他们在死刑问题上立场相左，但能友好收场。这种互动的特质预示着一种可持续的修辞：尽管存在分歧，但争论的方式为其他话题的商议留下了空间。这一互动展现了理性的两个重要要素——有能力、也愿意用证据支撑自身主张，并尊重对方。然而，它并未体现理性的其他关键构成。

如果在第二种互动中加入修辞论辩的专业知识，就可以转化为对**理性**的充分例证。其必备特征包括：论辩者展现出自己了解关于“不可杀人”与“正当报应”这两条圣经依据在历史上的各类立场；认识到与死刑相关的其他重要议题与论证（如法律的公正性与平等性）；并且能够表明自己熟知并能恰当检验己方与对方提出的各类论证。（此处我暂不提及理性的另一特征——遵从特定领域的推理规则，后文将对此展开论述。）在修改后的案例中，理性将体现为理由的运用与通情达理的态度，同时也表现为一种可持续、非操控性、双向、商议性、反思性且重视事实依据的修辞。这种修辞将展现好理由的逻辑在争论中应有的运用方式。

当我们将理性与通情达理视为高阶价值时，一个不可避免的问题会出现：一个人可以**具备理性却不通情达理**吗？我的回答是：若与他人论辩，则不能；若仅独自思考、自我推理，则可以。正如我此前所言，在与他人的互动中，一个人不可能既理性又不讲道理。通情达理是理性的一个维度，即**社会维度**。通情达理的前提是他人的存在。人们提出理由以说服他人，也只能对自身以外的人保持包容。包容是人际传播的特质，而非内心独白的特质。在人际互动中，一个人若不通情达理，就不能被视为完全理性。

如果我们认同通情达理是理性在人际论辩中的必要组成部分，就能形成一个关于**操控性修辞**的有效定义。所有修辞互动在某种意义上都具有操控性，因为传播者都带有信息意图，并且在选择立场、材料、篇章结构与表达风格时都具有策略性。换言之，每位传播者都力求为自身立场做出最有力的辩护。“操控性修辞”这一术语，应专门用于有证据表明受众被“利用”“摆布”，或为实现传播者自身目的而非受众自身目的而被驱使的情况。这类“操控”的表现包括：（1）以自利为目的，运用修辞策略、议题、论证形式、论证检验等专业知识；（2）表明传播者的意图并非维持对话、鼓励真实的双向交流、推进商议、接受真正的批判，或探求事件真相；（3）显示传播的功能在于服务个人野心，而非自我认知、社会认知或公共行动。

我并非暗示这些特征易于识别，也非认为可以快速、确定地判定某一传播者在某一时刻明显在操控受众。我想要阐明的是，**将观念适配于人**与**将人适配于观念**之间存在区别，设计话语将受众视为客体而非理性主体，二者同样存在本质差异。²⁵事实上，我们确实会基于一段时间的观察，判定某些传播者具有操控性。我的观点是，做出此类判断应当依据充分证据，证明传播者将受众当作实现自身私利的工具；“操控性修辞”这一表述应专指此类情形。²⁶

在主张人可以独自实现理性这一点上，我不认同约翰·哈德威格的立场，他认为理性只能通过人与人之间的对话互动得以体现。他坚持，只有在这类相遇中，人才能获得理性所要求的客观性。²⁷我的回应有两点。第一，他的立场预设内心独白不涉及他者。第二，这一立场忽视了一个事实：无论是内心独白还是外部对话，其理性思考所依赖的知识体系是一致的，即好理由逻辑中的评判性考量。这些考量具有“客观性”，因为它们由主体间建构并运用；每当面临修辞性决策时，它们都是一致且相关的要素；并且它们提供了一套系统方法，用以识别议题、相关数据与可靠推理。正如佩雷尔曼所指出的，个体可以被视为一种特殊受众，甚至能够化身“普遍受众”。当这一情况发生时，个体“便能享有理性所赋予的哲学特权”。²⁸

哈德威格混淆了**合理化**与**理性**。诚然，所有人都会时不时地进行自我合理化，无论是独处还是在他人面前，但同样真实的是，我们中的一些人（即便不是所有人）的推理方式足以让其决策被称为理性。具体而言：哈德威格的文章作为独白式传播的范例，体现了理性；而我们之间的交流作为对话式传播，同样反映了理性。

我的主张是，当个体无论独处还是在他人面前，其推理方式足以使其决策具备**理性能力**时，就可以证明他们运用了好理由逻辑的各项要素。也就是说，他们会运用适用于具体案例的程序性与评判性专业知识；掌握相关事实数据，评估支持与反对某项决策的论证，权衡影响事件的各项价值，并最终选择符合融贯性与逼真性检验的立场。在为自身立场辩护时，他们无论在独白还是对话互动中都会表现出通情达理。具备此类特征的推理是完整意义上的理性，也体现出高度的修辞能力，无论其所处的知识或行动领域为何。

熟悉亚里士多德《尼各马可伦理学》的读者可能会发现，好理由的逻辑（包括理性与通情达理概念）与亚里士多德的“实践智慧（phronesis）”概念存在相似之处。²⁹二者存在三重对应。第一，二者所预设的知识类型相似。与探究可被证明的必然性问题的科学、涵盖创造与实践领域的技艺不同，亚里士多德的实践智慧是“一种关乎人类事务、可进行商议的真实理性品质，其核心是践行对人而言的善”。³⁰它“关注人类事务与可商议的问题……拥有实践智慧的人最典型的功能就是出色商议，无人会商议必然如此之事，也无人会商议无行动目标之事，而目标必须是可通过行动实现的善……善于商议的人能够通过推理瞄准并达成人类可践行的至善”。³¹简而言之，好理由的逻辑与实践智慧共享着属于修辞学范畴的知识类型。

第二，我所提出的通情达理观念的核心，与亚里士多德的论述高度契合：“说一个人在实践智慧上具备良好判断力，意味着他善解人意、明辨事理，或能共情理解；因为公平之举是所有善良之人在与他人相处时共通的行为。”³²第三，好理由的逻辑与“实践智慧”在所要实现的目标上相一致。二者均将“正确理性”的发现与运用视为最高价值。正如亚里士多德所言：“正确理性由实践智慧所界定。”³³拥有实践智慧并运用好理由的逻辑，能让人在非严格科学或技艺范畴的生活领域中，做出恰当、审慎的明智抉择。亚里士多德认为实践智慧固有的特定智慧，关乎行动领域中的“真理”与“正当欲求相一致”。他指出：“理性必须肯定欲望所追求的事物。”³⁴因此，实践智慧与好理由逻辑的领域，正是伦理且有效的修辞实践。

一个人在特定专业领域中确立自身的理性，依靠的是了解并运用该领域固有的依据，遵从其特有的辩护规则。正如小亨利·约翰斯通所言，如果一个人展现出某一哲学流派的风格——其风格建立在以公认依据恰当立论、并遵守该流派论辩互动的既定规范之上——那么他就可以被视为该流派的内行。图尔敏、里克与贾尼克在《推理导论》一书中证明，这一普遍原则同样适用于法律、科学、艺术、伦理与企业管理等领域的推理。他们同时指出，依据与程序的变量会随时间变化。他们写道：

事实上，我们之所以能将科学、法律、管理等事业称为“理性”事业，既与它们在历史进程中自我革新的方式有关，也与它们在任一时期所采用的特定“理性标准”有关。自然科学之所以“理性”，并非因为科学家在某一时刻所持立场的真实性或稳固性，而是因为科学程序与原则在面对新经验与新观念时的“适应性”。……科学家对论辩的开放态度——必要时愿意修正甚至最根本的论证程序——正是其活动称得上真正具备理性的标志。³⁵

我认为，任何以推理为根基的其他领域，同样如此。

### **RATIONALITY AND RHETORICAL COMPETENCE**

If we cannot expect to find an invariant hierarchy of values that will reflect human choices in all communicative situations, must it follow that every communicative transaction will and must be governed by a unique, idiosyncratic set of values? I think not. In Western societies, at least, rationality is a transcendent value in any communication purporting to be or taken to be rhetorical. Accordingly, I maintain that rationality is an essential property of rhetorical competence. Rhetorical competence implies at the very least that one can invent content for communication, compose appropriate discourse for its communication, present a message in manners appropriate to the medium of communication, the audience, and the situation, and analyze and evaluate one’s own and others’ messages. To do these things with understanding of substance, method, and interactive relations, it is necessary to command what I have set forth as the logic of good reasons. One cannot be rhetorically competent without the ability to discern the presence, relevance, consequences, and consistency of values expressed or projected for expression within a specific rhetorical situation.

Being reasonable is more indicative of an attitude than an ability to participate effectively in communicative interactions. I do not mean to suggest that being reasonable is not a vital component of rhetorical transactions. I agree with Perelman’s stand that reasonableness is prerequisite to a pluralistic view of the world, that it is the force that transforms rationality into a human exercise of the mind. The idea of a democratic person, which is a presumption of nonmonolithic societies, is constituted by the concepts of reasonableness and rationality. As Perelman notes, insofar as rationality is conceived as adherence to formal logic, where major premises are mandated by fixed concepts of government, the result is a monistic system—a closed society. Rationality becomes the handmaiden of conservatism. The concept of rationality that I propose makes it an instrument for reasoned change. [24]

Being reasonable is an aspect of rationality, not its substance. To say that someone is reasonable within a democratic system is to say that that person uses reasons and is appreciative of and tolerant toward others who use reasons to justify their views. Being rational, on the other hand, implies not only that one respects reasoning, it also indicates that one knows the nature of argumentative issues, the forms of arguments and their tests, and the rules that govern the particular kind of argumentative interaction in which one may be an actor, whether scientific, political, legal, ethical, or other. One may be reasonable but not competent; one cannot be rational and incompetent.

To clarify this distinction further I want to consider a series of argumentative interactions. First, an instance in which A asserts: “Capital punishment is murder, clear and simple.” B responds: “It’s no such thing! Capital punishment is just retribution. Be reasonable.” A reacts by declaring: “What do you mean, be reasonable? You’re the one who is unreasonable.” Here we can see a popular conception of argument at work—a verbal and emotional disagreement. No statements justify claims, and the issue between the disputants becomes personal rather than substantive. Indeed, the implicit message of a demand that one be reasonable is that the person proposing an argument either (a) is ignorant, he or she does not know the facts of the case; or (b) is stupid, he or she has the facts but does not know how to draw proper inferences from them; or (c) has the facts, reasons from them well but does not begin with the proper assumptions or premises. The situation I have described is clearly an unreasonable one. It is not self-perpetuating, it is manipulative, it is not bilateral in any meaningful way, and it is not deliberative, reflexive, or attentive to data.

Second, an interaction in which A says: “Capital punishment is immoral; it denies the injunction that thou shalt not kill.” B replies: “That’s a reasonable position, but I disagree. I think capital punishment is a civil act, not the action of an individual rejecting the laws of the state. It is justified morally by the precept of an ‘eye for an eye.’” A responds: “I see what you are saying but I don’t agree that there is a difference between the actions of the state and of an individual when it comes to putting a person to death.” B counters: “Well, I guess we’ll just have to agree to disagree. I see no way to resolve our differences.” 

This encounter exhibits reasonableness; that is, the disputants offer justifications for their claims, they respect each other’s arguments, and they do not personalize the conflict. They part company on capital punishment, but do it amicably. The nature of their interaction implies a self-perpetuating rhetoric; that is, although they disagree, the manner of the disagreement invites deliberation on other topics. The interaction displays two important elements of rationality—the ability and willingness to support one’s claims with evidence and regard for the other. It does not, however, reveal other significant components of rationality.

Modified by indications of special knowledge in rhetorical argument, the second interaction can be transformed into an adequate illustration of rationality. The necessary features would include evidence that the arguers were aware of the historical positions that have been taken on the use of the biblical warrants of not killing and just retribution; recognition of other pertinent issues and arguments relative to capital punishment (such as the justice and equality of the law); and demonstration by the actors that they know and can apply the appropriate tests for each of the arguments they and their opponents put forth. (I do not mention here one further feature of rationality—the adherence to the rules of reasoning in a particular field. I shall have more to say of this feature later.) Rationality would be evident in the revised case by virtue of reasons and reasonableness and also by a rhetoric that is self-perpetuating, nonmanipulative, bilateral, deliberative, reflexive, and attentive to data. Such a rhetoric would reveal the logic of good reasons as it should be used in disputes.

A question that will inevitably arise when one considers rationality and reasonableness as superordinate values is: Can a person be rational without being reasonable? My answer is No, insofar as one is arguing with another; Yes, insofar as one is arguing, that is, reasoning within oneself. One cannot be rational and unreasonable in an interaction with another, as I have already implied. Being reasonable is an aspect of rationality, the social aspect. Being reasonable requires the presence of another. One offers reasons to persuade others and one can only be tolerant of someone other than oneself. Tolerance is a quality of interpersonal, not intrapersonal communication. One should not be considered fully rational in interpersonal interactions unless one is also reasonable.

If one accepts the view that being reasonable is a necessary constituent of rationality, where persons argue, one can develop a useful conception of what manipulative rhetoric is. All rhetorical interaction is manipulative in the sense that communicators intend messages, and all communicators are strategic in their chosen causes, selections of materials, designs of composition, and styles of presentation. Every communicator, in other words, seeks to make the best possible case for his or her position. “Manipulative rhetoric” is a term best reserved for use when there is evidence that the audience is being “played,” “worked,” or otherwise used for the communicator’s ends rather than for their own ends. Among the evidences of this kind of “manipulation” are: (1) uses of technical knowledge of rhetorical strategies and tactics, of issues, argumentative forms, tests of arguments, and so on in a self-serving way; (2) indications that the communicator’s aim is not to perpetuate dialogue, to encourage an authentic bilateral exchange, to further deliberation, to invite true criticism, or to advance the truth of the matter; and (3) clues that the function of the communication is to serve personal ambition rather than self-discovery or social knowledge or public action.

I do not mean to imply that these traits are always easy to identify, that one can quickly or surely claim that any given communicator at any given time is clearly manipulating the audience. I am arguing that there is a difference between adjusting ideas to people and people to ideas, and designing discourse to render people objects rather than rational persons. [25] It is a matter of fact that we do say, based on observing them over a period of time, that some communicators are manipulative. My point is that when we make such a claim, we should do so by virtue of evidence concerning the communicator’s treatment of the audience as a means to his or her own selfish ends; the phrase “manipulative rhetoric” should be reserved to designate such cases. [26]

In maintaining that one can be rational by oneself, I take exception to the stand of John Hardwig that rationality can be demonstrated only by persons interacting in a dialogue. He insists that only in such encounters can one achieve the objectivity that rationality requires. [27] My response is twofold. First, his position assumes that inner dialogue does not involve another. Second, the position ignores the fact that inner dialogue and outer dialogue rely on the same body of knowledge when they are rational—the criterial considerations of the logic of good reasons. These are “objective” matters in that they are constituted and used intersubjectively; they are consistent and relevant elements whenever a rhetorical decision is at hand; and they provide a methodical way to identify issues, relevant data, and sound reasoning. As Perelman points out, the individual person can be considered a kind of particular audience, one that even has the capacity to incarnate the “universal audience.” When this happens, the person “can enjoy the philosophic privilege conferred to reason.” [28]

Hardwig confuses rationalization and rationality. Certainly all individuals rationalize from time to time, alone and in the presence of others, but it is also true that some, if not all of us, reason in such a way that our decision making can be called rational. To put the point concretely: Hardwig’s essay, which is an example of monologic communication, exhibits rationality, and our exchange, which represents dialogic communication, also reflects rationality.

My claim is that when individuals, by themselves or in the presence of others, reason in such a way that their decision making can be called rationally competent, it can be shown that they will have used the elements of the logic of good reasons. By this I mean that they will have employed special knowledge of the issues, both procedural and evaluative, that apply in the given case; they will have informed themselves of relevant data, assessed the arguments that can be made for and against the decision, weighed the values that impinge on the matter, and decided to adopt a position that satisfies the tests of coherence and fidelity. In advocating their position, they will exhibit reasonableness in monologic and dialogic interactions. Reasoning that can be characterized in this way is fully rational and reflects a significant degree of rhetorical competence regardless of the field of knowledge or action.

One familiar with Aristotle’s Nicomachean Ethics may notice similarities between the logic of good reasons, including the concepts of rationality and reasonableness, and the Peripatetic’s notion of “practical wisdom” (phronesis). [29] There is a threefold parallel. First, in the kind of knowledge they presume. Unlike science, which investigates matters of necessity that can be demonstrated, and unlike art, which encompasses the realm of making and doing, Aristotle’s practical wisdom “‘is a truthful rational characteristic of acting in matters involving what is good for man.” [30] It is “concerned with human affairs and matters about which deliberation is possible. ... The most characteristic function of a man of practical wisdom is to deliberate well; no one deliberates about things that cannot be other than they are, nor about things that are not directed to some end, an end attainable by action. ... That man is good at deliberating who, by reasoning, can aim at and hit the best thing attainable to man by action.” [31] In short, the logic of good reasons and practical wisdom share that kind of knowledge which is the province of rhetoric.

Second, the essence of the idea of reasonableness that I have presented is caught in Aristotle’s statement that “to say that a person has good judgment in matters of practical wisdom implies that he is understanding and has good sense or that he has sympathetic understanding; for equitable acts are common to all good men in their relation with someone else.” [32] Third, the logic of good reasons and “practical wisdom” are alike in the kind of end they manifest. Both treat the discovery and application of “right reason” as superordinately valuable. As Aristotle observed, “right reason is that which is determined by practical wisdom.” [33] Possession of practical wisdom and use of the logic of good reasons yield intelligence in pursuit of proper and prudent conduct in those spheres of life that are not strictly matters of science or art. The specific sort of intelligence Aristotle marked as intrinsic to practical wisdom is that concerned with action where “truth” is “in harmony with correct desire.” As he said, “reasoning must affirm what desire pursues.” [34] The domain of practical wisdom and the logic of good reasons is, then, ethical and effective rhetorical performance.

One establishes one’s rationality in special fields by knowing and using the warrants indigenous to that field and adhering to the particular rules of advocacy followed in it. As Henry Johnstone, Jr., suggests, one may be considered competent in a school of philosophical thought if one exhibits its style, its style being fashioned by appropriate grounding of arguments on approved warrants and in respect to the accepted norms of argumentative interaction. In their book An Introduction to Reasoning, Toulmin, Rieke, and Janik demonstrated that the same general principle holds in regard to reasoning in such fields as law, science, art, ethics, and business management. They also point out that the variables of warrant and procedure will vary over time. They wrote:

> Indeed what makes it possible to speak of such enterprises as science and law and management as “rational” enterprises at all has as much to do with the ways in which they transform themselves in the course of their history as it does with the particular “rational standard” employed within them at any one time. It is not the truth or solidity of the positions adopted by scientists at any particular moment that makes the natural sciences “rational.” It is, rather, the “adaptability” of scientific procedures and principles in the face of new experience and new conceptions. ... It is the openness of scientists to argument—their readiness if need be, to revise even their most fundamental procedures of argument—that marks their activity off as an enterprise with some genuine claim to rationality. [35]

And the same may be said, I think, of any other field of endeavor that lives by reasoning.
