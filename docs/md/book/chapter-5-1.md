# PART III — NARRATIVE RATIONALITY, GOOD REASONS, AND AUDIENCES

## ASSESSING NARRATIVE FIDELITY: The Logic of Good Reasons

I introduced the concept of narrative rationality in Chapter 2 and described it as a form of rhetorical logic. I have discussed one of its principles—probability, or coherence—in some detail, but I have not yet examined the other component of narrative rationality: fidelity. The principle of coherence focuses on the integrity of a story as a whole, whereas the principle of fidelity concerns the individuated components of stories—whether they represent accurate assertions about social reality and thereby constitute good reasons for belief or action. The problem I pose and explore in this chapter is how the fidelity of reasons is to be assessed.

The approach I shall develop draws upon the work of Stephen Toulmin, Chaim Perelman, Gidon Gottlieb, Karl Wallace, and Wayne Booth <sup>1</sup>. To amplify and defend the notion of fidelity, I shall analyze the meaning of “logic” in the frequently used phrase *logic of good reasons*, reconstruct the concept of “good reasons,” present the design of a “logic of good reasons,” and suggest ways of implementing this logic using existing systems of logical assessment. The final question to be addressed is how, if at all, rationality or logic relates to rhetorical competence.

Several assumptions underlie my approach. First, I am convinced that the most indispensable need in contemporary rhetoric is a scheme by which values can be identified and their implications critically considered. This conviction stems from the belief that rhetorical communication is as laden with values as it is with what we call reasons. Humans, as rhetorical beings, are as much valuing as they are reasoning animals. I also maintain that value judgments are inevitable—they are not irrational; consensus about them will never be fully realized, and no analytically grounded hierarchy of values will ever achieve universal adherence. Finally, I believe rhetoricians have an obligation to inform students and raise the consciousness of citizens about the nature and functions of values, just as we have educated them about the nature and functions of reasons. As Gary Cronkhite has observed: “The best antidote for a sophistic rhetor is a sophisticated rhetoree, and we had best get at the business of providing such an antidote.” <sup>2</sup>
