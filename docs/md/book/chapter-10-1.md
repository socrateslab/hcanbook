# 10 回顾与反思
在古希腊哲学诞生之前，人类所有的传播形式都被理解为**神话与逻各斯、形式与内容、情感与理性**的统一体。没有哪一种传播形式被赋予特权，宣称自身具备传递知识、真理或现实的特殊能力。前苏格拉底哲学家开启了话语的“技术化”进程，但真正为下述观念奠定基础的是柏拉图，尤其是亚里士多德：唯有哲学话语——后来则是技术话语——才能为世界提供智慧与确定性。他们的后继者展开了激烈的思想论战，争论究竟哪一类话语（哲学、科学、修辞学或诗歌）有权主导、生成并评判思想，而哪一类只能承担次要职能，仅仅传递由其他领域创造的观念。

经由本书第一部分梳理的各个发展阶段，我们如今走到这样一个节点：来自不同学科的众多学者发现，既有的话语格局存在三重局限：（1）与研究对象的现实经验不符；（2）不足以解释或评判人类话语的整体形态；（3）无法充分说明话语被创造、感知与评价的心理过程。当下普遍存在一种迫切需求，即对人类话语形成更完善、更具包容性的理解。结构主义者提出，**形式**是衍生完整意义的核心现象；逻辑实证主义者主张，可验证的**内容**才是意义的核心；在文学理论、文学批评及部分社会科学中，被赋予不同定义的**文本**概念被视作核心框架，用以挖掘人类深层意义（或无意义）；现代哲学则提出了多种逻辑，包括非形式推理逻辑。当然，也有观点认为，既然不存在能够全面揭示意义的统摄性概念，且意义存在于理解者的主观认知之中，那么探寻传播意义的统一理念便是徒劳之举，人类最终只能陷入彻底的相对主义，甚至传播层面的无序状态。

本书试图针对这些思想挑战完成多项工作。首先，我提出，重新回顾并运用古希腊术语“逻各斯”的原初内涵具有重要价值。采纳这一视角，至少可以摆脱那种认为某一种人类传播形式垄断知识、真理与现实解释权的观念，或许能为审视人类传播提供一个全新起点。其次，我提出，**叙事**是人类观念最根本的概念结构。这并不意味着经由叙事传递的观念与情感优于其他话语形式，我只是主张：叙事是解读与评判一切传播行为的语境——它并非传播者刻意选择的话语模式，而是我们最初把握知识时天然呈现的形态。我们将自身的生活与文学理解为故事，这些故事又嵌套于历史、文化与人物的叙事之中，而所有叙事内部都蕴含着挣扎与冲突。再次，我主张叙事普遍存在于人类话语之中，并且我们会以理性的方式本能地回应这一层意义。正是在这一点上，我与传统修辞学和逻辑学分道扬镳。我并不否认这些传统至少对部分人类传播现象具有解释力，人类确实会有意识地为观念和情感构建结构，包括引言、高潮、十四行诗体、史诗体例、形式化论证类型等各类传播结构。但我与修辞、逻辑及诗学传统的不同之处在于：我认为，在人类传播被赋予的任何结构背后，**叙事的感知框架始终在约束并生成意义**，观念与情感永远会在时间流中被感知与理解。基于这一基本立场，本书致力于阐释并例证我们如何区分优质与欠优的叙事。我提出，人类具备一种“叙事理性”，每当我们创造或接受传播内容时，都会不自觉地运用并检验这种理性。

修辞学传统为存在争议的场景提供了若干理性概念，这些概念具备清晰、系统的理性评判标准。我们应当检验“论证路径”，判断其是否与争议议题相关；在所有可讨论的论题中，论辩双方所选择的内容是否最切题、最具说服力。古典修辞传统为此提供了思路，它强调在争议中，论证与反驳会形成“争议点”——即陷入僵局的节点，必须先解决这些问题，论证才能继续推进并形成判断。这便是我们所继承的“修辞逻辑”的核心。本书并非要否定这种逻辑的价值，而是试图揭示并阐释一种支配所有话语的逻辑，无论这些话语是否表现为显性论证。我认为，这种逻辑的两大原则是**融贯性**与**可靠性**。我们无需接受正规训练，便会自然而然地追问任何一种叙事是否“自圆其说”，是否构成对现实的可靠表述；我们会判断其是否与我们已知并相信的相关叙事相符。基于这些标准，我们要么认同某一叙事（及其作者），要么将其视为谬误。当我们发现故事或叙事提供了可供接受的“充分理由”时，便会选择认同。所谓充分理由，是人类话语或行为中能够支撑信念与行动的依据；而理由之所以充分，是因为它们被认为：（1）符合并契合我们的认知与价值；（2）适用于待决的判断；（3）对自身与他人具有积极的预期效果；（4）与我们所信奉的理想行为准则一致。

我相信，每当人们接触到暗含知识、真理或现实主张的叙事时，这一逻辑便会发挥作用。除此之外，若话语采用了特定形式，源自传统的其他逻辑（如修辞逻辑）也可相应适用。本书所阐释并运用于政治、审美与哲学话语评析的这一逻辑，具有普遍性，因此可作为人类话语的范式。**叙事范式**是构建完整修辞理论所需的基础，这一理论并不会取代从属逻辑，而是将其整合进一套更具包容性的框架之中，用以解释符号讯息的创造、构思、适配、表达与接受。

### **10 IN RETROSPECT**

Before the advent of philosophy in ancient Greece, all modes of human communication were regarded as *mythos*/*logos*, form/content, and feeling/reason. No instance of human communication was privileged over another as having a special capacity to convey knowledge, truth, or reality. The pre-Socratics began the “technologizing” of discourse, but it was Plato and especially Aristotle who set the foundations for the view that only philosophical, later technical, discourse could provide wisdom and certainty in the world. Their successors fought intellectual battles over which genre of discourse—philosophy, science, rhetoric, or poetic—had the right to preside over and generate and evaluate ideas, and which genre should be assigned the lesser tasks of supervising the communication of ideas created elsewhere.

Through stages that have been traced in Part I of this book, we have arrived at a point where a host of scholars from diverse disciplines find this situation (a) inaccurate in regard to the data before them, (b) inadequate in explaining or assessing the totality of human discourse, and (c) inadequate in accounting for the mental processes by which discourse is created, perceived, and evaluated. There is a widespread exigence for an improved, more comprehensive understanding of discourse. Structuralists have offered form as the seminal phenomenon from which comprehensive meaning flows. Logical positivists proposed verifiable content as the heart of meaning. In literary theory and criticism and some of the social sciences, the concept of text, variously defined, is offered as the comprehensive concept by means of which the depths of human meaning (or nonmeaning) can be plumbed. In modern philosophy multiple logics, including logics of informal reasoning, have been proposed. And, of course, there have been those who have proposed that since no overarching concept will be comprehensively revealing and since meaning is in the eye of the beholder, the search for unifying notions about communicative meaning is a useless enterprise and there is no escape from utter relativism or even from communicative anarchy.

This book has been an attempt to do several things in response to these intellectual challenges. First, I have proposed that there is merit in recalling and making use of the original meanings attached to the ancient term “*logos*.” To adopt this view at least escapes the notion of a single mode of human communication that has exclusive jurisdiction over knowledge, truth, and reality; it perhaps provides a fresh start for looking at human communication. Second, I have proposed that narration is the foundational, conceptual configuration of ideas for our species. This does not imply that narrated ideas and feelings are better than those conveyed through other modes of discourse. I simply take the position that narration is the context for interpreting and assessing all communication—not a mode of discourse laid on by a creator’s deliberate choice but the shape of knowledge as we first apprehend it. We interpret our lives and our literature as stories that emerge within other stories of history, culture, and character—within all of which struggles and conflicts inhere. Third, I have contended that narration is omnipresent in human discourse and that we innately respond to that aspect of meaning rationally. It is at this point that I have begun to diverge from rhetorical and logical traditions. I have not denied that those traditions offer conceptions that are true to at least some human communication. There are deliberately conceived structures that can be and are imposed on ideas and feelings. They include introductions, climaxes, sonnet form, epic form, types of formal argumentation, and other structurations of communication. I have diverged from rhetorical, logical, and poetic traditions, however, by claiming that behind any structure that is given to human communication, the perceptual framework of narration will always also be constraining and projecting meaning. Ideas and feelings will always be sensed in and through time. Because I hold this basic view, I have undertaken in this book to conceptualize and illustrate how we discriminate good from less good accounts. I have argued that there is, for our species, such a thing as “narrative rationality” and that we all understand and test for this rationality whenever we create or experience communication.

The rhetorical tradition has given us some concepts of rationality that apply where there is dispute. Those concepts provide clear and systematic tests of rationality. “Lines of argument” should be tested to discover whether or not they are relevant to the issues in dispute and whether or not, of all the topics that might be discussed, those chosen by competing arguers were the most pertinent and the strongest the arguers could select in the immediate case. Classical tradition in rhetoric provides assistance in making this judgment by stressing that, in controversies, arguments and counterarguments come to “stands” or *stases*—points of impasse that must be resolved before argumentation can continue toward decision. This is the essence of “rhetorical logic” as we have inherited it. My concern in this book has not been to deny the usefulness of this logic but to discover and illustrate a logic that governs discourse whether it is transparently argumentative or not. The two major principles of such logic are, I believe, coherence and fidelity. We naturally and without formal instruction ask about any account of any sort whatever whether or not it “holds together” and adds up to a reliable claim to reality. We ask whether or not an account is faithful to related accounts we already know and believe. On these terms we identify with an account (and its author) or we treat it as mistaken. We identify with stories or accounts when we find that they offer “good reasons” for being accepted. Good reasons are elements in human discourse or performance that we take as warrants for belief or action. Reasons are good when they are perceived as (1) true to and consistent with what we think we know and what we value, (2) appropriate to whatever decision is pending, (3) promising in effects for ourselves and others, and (4) consistent with what we believe is an ideal basis for conduct.

This is the logic that I believe comes into play when anyone experiences an account that implies claims about knowledge, truth, or reality. Beyond it, other logics, such as the rhetorical logics we have inherited from tradition, come into play if they are appropriate to the special forms we sometimes give to ideas. The logic I have outlined and critically applied in interpreting and assessing political, aesthetic, and philosophical discourse is, I believe, a universal logic. As such, it is paradigmatic of human discourse. The narrative paradigm is the foundation on which a complete theory of rhetoric needs to be built. To do so would not displace subordinate logics. It would incorporate them within a comprehensive explanation of the creation, composition, adaptation, presentation, and reception of symbolic messages.

#### **3. 核心理念回顾 **



在这部分总结中，费舍尔确立了三个关键支柱：
1.  **叙事是基础**：叙事不仅仅是一种文学体裁，它是人类感知和组织知识的**根本形式**。我们通过故事来理解世界。
2.  **叙事理性（Narrative Rationality）**：不同于形式逻辑，叙事理性基于两个维度：
    * **连贯性（Coherence）**：故事内部是否逻辑自洽？人物行为是否符合设定？
    * **忠实性（Fidelity）**：故事是否与听众已有的经验、价值观和对他人的认知相符？
3.  **修辞学的未来**：叙事范式并不是要取代传统的修辞逻辑（如论辩、证据测试），而是要将其**整合**进一个更宏大的框架中，解释人类如何接收和处理所有的符号信息。
