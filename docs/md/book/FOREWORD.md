# 前言
从“人类本质上是叙事者”这一前提出发，沃尔特·R.费希尔提出，对人类所有形式的传播进行解读与评判，最有效的视角是**叙事视角**。他认为，在我们习得并运用的各类话语结构之下——如论证、说明、戏剧、小说等——人类始终遵循着一套**叙事逻辑**。费希尔指出，自幼年起，我们便会依据经验中已有的故事，去解读和评判新的故事；我们会审视新的叙事是否忠实于我们所知或自认为所知的现实，考察其内部与外部是否融贯。尽管后来我们会掌握更为复杂的标准来评判叙事的可靠性与连贯性，但将话语作为“故事”进行建构、解读与评判，依然是人类首要的、与生俱来的、独属于物种层面的“逻辑”。

近年来，形式逻辑原则不足以解释非形式理性与人类价值判断的问题已得到广泛证实，尤其是在斯蒂芬·图尔明、柴姆·佩雷尔曼、道格拉斯·埃宁格、韦恩·布罗克里德等学者关于论证理论的研究中。费希尔以这些学者以及其他杰出理论家（尤其是肯尼斯·伯克与阿拉斯戴尔·麦金太尔）的成果为基础，详细阐释了一种全新的逻辑，该逻辑与他将人类传播视为叙事的核心观念相契合。他提出了一套比现有论证理论更为宽广的逻辑过程观念。费希尔认为，从修辞学视角将任何人类传播形式视为对态度、信念或行为的引导时，**叙事理性**都是理解与评判这些传播形式的基础。

在过去十年间，费希尔对其总体理论框架的诸多特征与应用进行了系统阐述与论证。相关研究得到了修辞学研究者与关注意向性传播的哲学家的广泛赞誉。在《作为叙事的人类传播》一书中，费希尔将其多年广泛阅读与分析的成果整合为一套统一的“论证”或“叙事”。他在书中全面呈现了自身的理论主张，将其置于西方修辞学与逻辑学理论发展史的语境之中，并通过分析多种不同“形式”或“体裁”的话语，例证了其理论体系的批判价值。

《作为叙事的人类传播》一书补充并挑战了近几十年来修辞学领域诸多重要研究成果。通过构建一套逻辑体系，费希尔的研究超越了伯克的戏剧主义；通过将该逻辑奠基于叙事之上，他拓展并深化了佩雷尔曼的实践推理路径。有关“非形式逻辑”与“论证理论”的修辞学与哲学研究，也被纳入费希尔的哲学—批判体系之中，其核心前提是：论证形式始终与普遍存在的叙事逻辑相伴而生，并作为其补充而存在。当代学界对话语“通用形式”的界定探索——相关研究可见于南卡罗来纳大学出版社修辞学/传播系列出版的《形式、文类与政治话语研究》——在费希尔的理论体系中同样具有位置，前提是承认叙事逻辑的基本原则贯穿于所有文类之中。同样，费希尔的传播分析也吸纳并整合了当代有关文体学、传播角色设定、神话制约影响以及符号引导本质的研究成果。他主张，所有此类传播特征与功能，均源于人类探究叙事融贯性及其与已知现实契合度的先天冲动，并在此基础上发展演化。

沃尔特·费希尔这部极具原创性的著作，值得所有关注实践传播理论与批判研究的学者认真研读。

卡罗尔·C.阿诺德
南卡罗来纳大学出版社

**FOREWORD**

Beginning with the assumption that humans are essentially storytellers, Walter R. Fisher proposes that all forms of human communication are most usefully interpreted and assessed from a narrational perspective. He believes that beneath the learned and imposed structures by means of which we give discourse such forms as “argument,” “exposition,” “drama,” and “fiction,” the human species is always pursuing a narrative logic. From infancy, Fisher argues, we interpret and evaluate new stories against older stories acquired through experience. We search new accounts for their faithfulness to what we know, or think we know, and for their internal and external coherence. Later we learn more sophisticated criteria and standards for assessing a story’s fidelity and coherence, but constructing, interpreting, and evaluating discourse as “story” remains our primary, innate, species-specific “logic.”

That principles of formal logic inadequately explain informal rationality and human valuing has been well established in recent years, especially through studies in argumentation by such writers as Stephen Toulmin, Chaim Perelman, Douglas Ehninger, and Wayne Brockriede. Building on their work, as well as on that of other distinguished writers, especially Kenneth Burke and Alasdair MacIntyre, Fisher details a new logic, one appropriate to his basic conception of human communication as narration. He proposes a conception of logical processes that is broader than theory of argument has acknowledged to date. Fisher contends that “narrative rationality” underlies understanding and evaluation of any form of human communication that is viewed rhetorically, as an inducement to attitude, belief, or action.

During the past decade, Fisher has presented and argued for several features and applications of his general position. That work has received plaudits from both rhetoricians and philosophers interested in intentional communication. In *Human Communication as Narration*, Fisher now draws together into a single “argument” or “story” the results of his extensive reading and analyses. Here, he presents his theoretical claims in comprehensive form, places them in their context *vis-à-vis* the history of rhetorical and logical theory in the West, and illustrates the critical usefulness of his formulations by applying them in analyzing several different “forms” or “genres” of discourse.

*Human Communication as Narration* supplements and challenges major contributions made by other students of rhetoric in the last several decades. By providing a logic, his work moves beyond Burke’s dramatism; by grounding this logic in narration, he broadens and deepens Perelman’s approach to practical reasoning. Rhetorical and philosophical research on “informal logic” and “argumentation” is incorporated within Fisher’s philosophical/critical system with the simple caveat that argumentative forms occur only together with and in addition to a ubiquitous narrative logic. Contemporary attempts to identify “generic forms” of discourse— the subject of *Form, Genre, and the Study of Political Discourse*, published by the University of South Carolina Press in its Rhetoric/Communication Series—have their place within Fisher’s system, provided the basic principles of narrative logic are recognized as operating within all genres. In like ways, Fisher’s analysis of communication accepts and incorporates contemporary research on stylistics, on the assumption of roles in communication, on the influences of mythic conditioning, and on the nature of symbolic inducement. Fisher’s claim is that all such communicative features and functions arise from and evolve out of human beings’ innate impulses to explore the coherence of any account and its fidelity to the known.

Walter Fisher’s highly original book merits close attention from all persons concerned with the theory and criticism of practical communication.

**Carroll C. Arnold**
Editor, Rhetoric/Communication
University of South Carolina Press
