# 受众的哲学、政治与个人特质

佩雷尔曼理论所认定的受众（及倡导者）应具备的首要特质，是秉持**多元主义信念**，并将其贯穿于对现实、真理、社会本质与自我的认知之中。认同佩雷尔曼新修辞学的倡导者与受众，会和他一道拒斥以下思想流派所体现的观念：巴门尼德与柏拉图的形而上学、弗朗西斯·培根与约翰·洛克的经验主义、笛卡尔与斯宾诺莎的理性主义，以及维也纳学派的逻辑实证主义。他们同样会摒弃休谟与诸多当代结构主义者的怀疑论。他们会追随佩雷尔曼，反抗“各类绝对主义所秉持的、不容妥协也不可消解的对立观念：理性与想象的二元对立、知识与意见的二元对立、无可辩驳的自明性与具有欺骗性的意志的二元对立、普遍公认的客观性与不可通约的主观性的二元对立、对所有人具有约束力的现实与纯粹个体性价值的二元对立”。¹²新修辞学的信奉者会主张，人类选择与行动的领域向理性与正义开放，在这一生活范畴内，无人拥有不容置疑的绝对立场。

在真理问题上，遵循佩雷尔曼新修辞学的人会认同：“不可否认，在诸多知识领域中，真理的理想必须凌驾于其他考量之上。”但另一方面，他们也会坚持，“当我们关乎行动、判断何为正义或不义、善或恶、应当倡导或禁止之事时”，起支配作用的必须是**论证理性**，而非证明理性。¹³佩雷尔曼认为，“真理理念唯有在控制与验证方法能够恰当运用的范围内，才可作为观点的评判标准，而不能以真理之名强行推行值得质疑的意识形态”。¹⁴因此，这类受众会承认真理的存在及其作用——真理在部分知识领域是决定性因素，也是论证的组成部分，但并非合理行动选择的终极决定因素。

上述对现实与真理的看法，暗含了佩雷尔曼新修辞学所指向的若干政治要求，其中核心是对**自由开放社会**的预设。佩雷尔曼认为，“意见自由表达、各类观点公开讨论的民主体制，是仅作为合理性存在的实践理性得以运用的必要配套条件”。¹⁵佩雷尔曼的修辞理论与奉行一元论意识形态的社会互不相容。在这类体制中，当官方治理者无法“让所有人信服其观点的真理性时，便会以上帝、理性、真理之名，或以国家、政党利益之名，为强制与武力镇压反抗者辩护”。¹⁶与之相对，多元主义国家倡导“尊重个体与诸多时而合作、时而对立的群体”。因此，多元社会支持“信仰自由、新闻自由、集会与结社自由”。¹⁷维系多元社会活力的内在核心，正是实践哲学——即新修辞学。若缺少这一哲学，“人会沦为自身无意识的玩物，甚至成为文化、政治与宗教传统的奴隶……既无法超越这些传统，也无法使其适应新的需求与情境”。¹⁸

要避免这些缺陷、坚守多元主义，个体必须“通情达理”，即在“判断与行为中受常识指引”的人。¹⁹因此，“通情达理”是佩雷尔曼所构想的、运用新修辞学的倡导者与受众的标志性特质。通情达理的人包容异见，愿意倾听他人观点，甚至“最终接纳对方立场”，心怀他人、谦逊审慎、尽责诚实、公正仁慈、慷慨大度。²⁰由此可见，佩雷尔曼的新修辞学并非为狂热分子或严格理性主义者构建的哲学。它不否定理性，而是强调“正是理性与合理性的辩证运动、逻辑融贯性与结论悖理性质的对质，构成了思想进步的根基”。²¹通情达理的人坚守正义，这份坚守也将确保思想进步具有人文温度，这一点我将在后文阐明。

佩雷尔曼支持多元主义的立场，在其关于修辞学处理“模糊概念”之作用的观点中得到了具体体现。模糊概念包含前文提及的诸多理念：现实、真理、自由、理性与正义。“修辞学的使命……是让我们抵御对模糊概念的滥用。正是通过对论证、修辞与辩证程序的研习，我们才能学会区分合理推理与诡辩推理，区分旨在说服与信服的推理，与意在欺骗、误导他人的推理。”基于这一信念，佩雷尔曼主张将修辞学“作为通识教育的核心内容”进行教授。²²这类教育将在哲学、政治领域以及个人观念与行为层面培育多元主义，塑造出兼具民主体制美德、尤其是坚守理性与正义的倡导者与受众。

### **PHILOSOPHICAL, POLITICAL, AND PERSONAL CHARACTERISTICS OF AUDIENCES**

The first characteristic of audiences (and advocates) that Perelman’s theory identifies as desirable is a commitment to pluralism that pervades one’s view of reality, truth, the nature of society, and oneself. Advocates as well as audiences who subscribe to Perelman’s new rhetoric will reject, along with Perelman, the perspective reflected in the metaphysics of Parmenides and Plato, the empiricism of Francis Bacon and John Locke, the rationalism of Descartes and Spinoza, and the logical positivism of the Vienna School. They will also dismiss the skepticism of Hume and many contemporary structuralists. They will join in Perelman’s combat against “uncompromising and irreducible oppositions presented by all kinds of absolutisms: dualisms of reason and imagination, of knowledge and opinion, of irrefutable self-evidence and deceptive will, of a universally accepted objectivity and an incommunicable subjectivity, of a reality binding on everybody and values that are purely individual.” [12] Adherents of the new rhetoric will hold that the world of human choice and action is open to reason and justice, that in this sphere of life, no one commands an indisputable position.

In regard to truth, persons acting in line with Perelman’s new rhetoric will agree that “It is undeniable that in a great many areas of knowledge the ideal of truth must prevail over other considerations.” On the other hand, they will also insist that “when we are concerned with action, knowing what is just or unjust, good or bad, what to encourage or forbid,” it is argumentative, not demonstrative, reason that must rule. [13] “The idea of truth,” Perelman maintained, “should serve as a criterion for opinions only to the extent that the techniques of control and verification allow it to be appropriately used without attempting to impose questionable ideologies in the name of truth.” [14] The audience will thus recognize the existence and the role of truth as a decisive factor in some areas of knowledge and as a component in argument, but not as an ultimate determinant in reasonable choice of action.

Entailed in these views of reality and truth are certain political requirements implied by Perelman’s new rhetoric. Chief among them is the presumption of a free and open society. “The democratic regime of free expression of opinions, of open discussion of all viewpoints,” Perelman believed, “is the indispensable concomitant for the usage of the practical reason that is simply reasonable.” [15] Perelman’s rhetorical theory is incompatible with societies driven by monistic ideologies. In such systems, when the official agents of government fail in “persuading everybody of the truth of their point of view, they may justify coercion and the use of force against the recalcitrants in the name of God, of reason, of truth, or of the State’s or party’s interest.” [16] A pluralistic state, on the other hand, fosters “respect for individuals and for the many groups which sometimes cooperate with each other and sometimes oppose one another.” Pluralistic societies, therefore, support “freedom of belief, freedom of the press, and freedom of meeting and association.” [17] Intrinsic to the vitality of pluralistic societies is practical philosophy—the new rhetoric. Without such a philosophy, “man becomes a plaything of his unconscious, or even a slave of cultural, political and religious traditions which ... he can neither transcend nor adapt to new needs and situations.” [18]

To avoid these failings and to be true to pluralism, one must be “reasonable,” a person who in “judgments and conduct is influenced by common sense.” [19] Being reasonable is thus a defining trait of advocates and audiences who employ the new rhetoric as Perelman conceived it. The reasonable person is tolerant of dissent, willing to listen to the other and perhaps “eventually to accept his point of view,” concerned for others, modest, humble, prudent, responsible, honest, impartial, merciful, and generous. [20] Perelman’s new rhetoric is therefore not a philosophy for fanatics or strict rationalists. It does not deny rationality but insists that “It is the dialectic of the rational and the reasonable, the confrontation of logical coherence with the unreasonable character of conclusions, which is the basis of the progress of thought.” [21] The reasonable person is committed to justice and this commitment ensures that such progress will be humane, as I shall show shortly.

The import of Perelman’s position in support of pluralism is given precise application in his view of the role of rhetoric in dealing with “confused notions.” Confused notions include several of the notions I have already mentioned: reality, truth, freedom, reason, and justice. “It is the role of rhetoric ... to arm us against the abusive use of confused notions. It is through the study of argumentational, rhetorical and dialectical procedures that we learn to distinguish acceptable reasoning from sophistical reasoning, to distinguish reasoning in which one seeks to persuade and to convince, from reasoning in which one seeks to deceive and to lead into error.” Because he believed this, Perelman advocated teaching rhetoric “as a principal element of any liberal education.” [22] Such teaching would promote pluralism in philosophy, politics, and one’s personal outlook and behavior. It would be productive of advocates and audiences who characteristically exhibit the virtues of democratic regimes, especially reason and justice.
