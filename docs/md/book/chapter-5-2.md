

## THE MEANING OF “LOGIC”

The first issue to be addressed is the meaning of “logic” in the expression *logic of good reasons*. By “logic,” I do not mean the study of the principles or structures that constitute a concept or activity, as in the “logic of the humanities” or the “logic of legal controversy.” Nor do I mean a formal system, such as a deontic logic that seeks to reveal the structures of reasoning about matters of permission and obligation. The general inappropriateness of formal logic for rhetorical reasoning has been well demonstrated by Toulmin³ and Perelman⁴.

Here, I use “logic” to designate a systematic set of procedures designed to aid in the analysis and assessment of elements of reasoning in rhetorical interactions. The procedures I propose consist of a series of criterial questions intended to reveal the role of values in practical reasoning and to provide a foundation for their evaluation. This scheme serves as an adjunct to existing “logics” used in the study of rhetorical reasoning. It is an informal (noncategorical) system, coordinated with the type of logic that dominates argumentation textbooks—the classification of types of reasoning (sign, causal, analogical, etc.) along with their appropriate evaluative tests.
