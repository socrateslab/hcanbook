# 从哲学走向技术性话语
在前苏格拉底哲学家、柏拉图与亚里士多德之后，主张哲学话语高于其他一切话语形式的理论中，最具影响力的当属弗朗西斯·培根。事实上，培根思想的真正影响，是将**科学（技术、说明性）话语**抬升至所有话语之上，甚至包括哲学。哲学虽仍保有崇高地位，但仅限其聚焦于科学研究的范畴。哲学的“降格”与新的知识观相伴而生：知识关乎物质世界，且必须是严格经验性的。这在逻辑上实现了一次转向——从重视演绎推理转为重视归纳推理。认知领域的新权威不再是亚里士多德或教会，而是**方法**，即严谨的经验研究程序。

培根思想带来的重要影响之一，是对**修辞发明**的重新界定。古典理论认为，修辞学家通过论题法考察既定主题下已知或可信的内容，从而发现或然性论点。而培根对修辞发明的理解是：它仅仅是对非修辞过程所发现的知识进行加工，或寻找适配传播的表达方式。其思想的结果是将修辞学降格为一门“管理性技艺”，即其功能仅在于辅助传递由其他学科规范研究所获的知识。最早对这种修辞观进行系统阐释的是乔治·坎贝尔的《修辞哲学》。休·布莱尔与理查德·惠特利的修辞学著作进一步推动了这一观念的普及。

在更为狭隘的形式下，这种管理型修辞观经由亚历山大·贝恩在英国得以推广，其教材《英语写作与修辞学》应用极为广泛；在美国则以哈佛大学的亚当斯·谢尔曼·希尔为代表，他的《修辞学原理》成为美国标准教科书。二人均坚持，修辞学仅研究散文写作形式，与内容毫无关联。

培根为新科学扫清了道路，而笛卡尔则规定了其研究路径。笛卡尔的贡献在于，将经验研究方法建立在数学证明的基础之上，使之趋于完善。他虽“高度推崇雄辩，热爱诗歌”，却认为这些是“自然的禀赋，而非学习的成果”。其他学问因立足于传统哲学而遭到摒弃，因为传统哲学容纳多元观点，而“其中最多只能有一种是正确的”。

笛卡尔观点的最终结果，是催生了**逻辑实证主义**学说：任何陈述唯有在原则上可被经验证实，或包含逻辑蕴含关系时，方能宣称表达了知识。该学说同时认为价值属于“无意义”范畴。如此一来，技术专家的话语被定为人类唯一严肃的传播形式；修辞与诗性话语则被视作非理性的人际互动形式，即便偶尔有趣，也不足为道。

为培根与笛卡尔的思想推波助澜的还有约翰·洛克，其目标在于确立：知识“唯有在观念与事物实在相符合时才是真实的”。与前人一样，洛克抨击三段论、论题法（修辞发明的指引）以及一切华丽辞藻。在《人类理解论》中他写道：“……如果我们要如实地谈论事物，就必须承认，除了条理与清晰之外，修辞学的所有技艺，一切由雄辩术人为创造的比喻性言辞，别无他用，只为灌输错误观念、煽动情感，进而误导判断，因此实属彻头彻尾的欺骗。”如此一来，唯有说明性话语才是学术研究与传播的合法形式。

关于科学传播的理想形态，或许没有比托马斯·斯普拉特的表述更具启发性的了。在否定修辞与诗性话语之后，他宣称，这种“新”传播形式将回归“原初的纯粹与简练，人类过去表达诸多事物，所用言辞几乎与事物数量相当”。其风格应当是“紧凑、直白、自然的表达方式；确切的措辞；清晰的意义；质朴的流畅：让一切尽可能接近数学般的明晰”。从中不难看出对二十世纪普通语义学的影响。

如今，这些观点所带来的后果正引发广泛讨论：否定价值作用的知识观、逻辑与日常话语的割裂、“专家”及其话语的特权化。作为对这些倾向的正面反驳，叙事范式便是典型例证。这些实证主义观点也遭到众多哲学家的批判，包括理查德·伯恩斯坦、汉斯-格奥尔格·伽达默尔、尤尔根·哈贝马斯、理查德·罗蒂以及卡尔文·施拉格等。事实上，并非只有人文学者一直在质疑这些观念。在库尔特·哥德尔与维尔纳·海森堡对科学确定性提出挑战之后，科学家与科学哲学家也加入了讨论。斯蒂芬·图尔敏的《回归宇宙学》与弗里乔夫·卡普拉的《转折点》便展现了这一反思的方向。我们无法预测这场争论的结果，但可以期待一种接近古人的逻各斯观念重新出现——那种视所有人类及其传播为理性、值得尊重的观念。

### FROM PHILOSOPHY TO TECHNICAL DISCOURSE

After the pre-Socratics, Plato, and Aristotle, the next most influential statement of the view that philosophical discourse reigns supreme over other forms of discourse is that of Francis Bacon. Actually, the effect of Bacon’s thought was to elevate scientific (technical, expository) discourse over all forms of discourse, including philosophy. Philosophy retained a high status, but only as it focused on science. The “demotion” of philosophy was a concomitant of the new theory of knowledge—that knowledge concerns the physical world and is strictly empirical. This was a reversal in logic from an emphasis on deduction to emphasis on induction. The new authority on knowing was not Aristotle or the church but method, the procedures for proper empirical investigation. One of the major results of Bacon’s ideas was a reconception of rhetorical invention. The ancient theory had it that rhetoricians discover probabilities by considering, topically, what is known or can be believed about a given subject. Bacon’s conception of rhetorical invention was that it is simply processing or finding communicative adaptations of knowledge originally discovered by nonrhetorical processes. The effect of his thought was to reduce rhetoric to a “managerial art”; that is, its function was to facilitate transmission of knowledge acquired through investigations regulated by other disciplines. The earliest full exposition of rhetoric so conceived was George Campbell’s *The Philosophy of Rhetoric*.⁶ The rhetorical writings of Hugh Blair⁷ and Richard Whately⁸ helped to popularize this view. In still narrower form, the managerial conception of rhetoric was popularized in Great Britain by Alexander Bain, whose manual, *English Composition and Rhetoric*,⁹ was very widely used, and in the United States by Adams Sherman Hill of Harvard, whose *Principles of Rhetoric*¹⁰ became a standard textbook in America. Both men insisted that rhetoric was the study of the forms of prose composition and had nothing at all to do with content.

Bacon cleared the field for the new science, but Descartes determined how it was to be plowed. Descartes’s contribution was to perfect the method of empirical investigation by grounding it on mathematical demonstration. He esteemed “eloquence highly, and loved poetry,” yet he felt that “they were gifts of nature rather than fruits of study.”¹¹ Other studies were rejected because they were based on traditional philosophy, which allowed a diversity of opinions, where “no more than one of them can ever be right.”¹² The eventual result of Descartes’s views was the doctrine of the logical positivists, who held that no statement could claim expression of knowledge unless it was empirically verifiable—at least in principle—or it involved a logical entailment. The doctrine also entailed the notion that values were “non-sense.” The discourse of technical experts was thereby designated as the only serious form of human communication; rhetoric and poetic were considered irrational, if sometimes amusing, forms of human transaction.

Aiding and abetting the general influence of Bacon and Descartes was John Locke, whose aim was to establish that knowledge is “real only so far as there is a conformity between ideas and the reality of things.”¹³ Like his predecessors, Locke attacked the value of the syllogism, the topics (guides to rhetorical invention), and all forms of ornamental speech. In *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*, he wrote: “... if we would speak of things as they are, we must allow, that all the Art of Rhetorick besides Order and Clearness, all the artificial figurative application of Words Eloquence hath invented, are for nothing else, but to insinuate wrong Ideas, move the Passions, and thereby mislead Judgment; and so indeed are perfect cheat.”¹⁴ Thus the only form of discourse for learned study and communication was exposition.

There is, perhaps, no more instructive statement of the ideal form of scientific communication than that of Thomas Sprat. After dismissing rhetoric and poetic, he declared that the “new” form of communication would return “to the primitive purity, and shortness, when men deliver’d so many things, almost in an equal number of words.” The style was to be a “close, naked, natural way of speaking; positive expressions; clear sense; a native easiness: bringing all things as near Mathematical plainness, as they can.”¹⁵ It is not difficult to see in this statement an impetus to twentieth-century general semantics.

Today there is much ferment about the consequences of these views: the concept of knowledge that denies a role for values, the separation of logic from everyday discourse, and the privileging of “experts” and their discourse. The narrative paradigm, as an affirmative proposal against these moves, is a case in point. These positivistic views have also been attacked by a host of philosophers, including Richard Bernstein,¹⁶ George-Hans Gadamer,¹⁷ Jürgen Habermas,¹⁸ Richard Rorty,¹⁹ and Calvin Schrag.²⁰ Indeed, it is not humanists alone who have been and are questioning these ideas. Following the challenges by Kurt Gödel and Werner Heisenberg to scientific certainty, scientists and philosophers of science have joined the discussion. The direction of this rethinking is illustrated by Stephen Toulmin’s *Return to Cosmology*²¹ and Fritjof Capra’s *Turning Point*.²² One cannot predict the outcome of the arguments, but one can hope for a concept of *logos* that approximates that of the ancients. Theirs was a concept that regarded all humans and their communication as not irrational and as deserving respect.
